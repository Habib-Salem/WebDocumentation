Introduction

The purpose of this web documentation report is to effectively provide users a justification and rationale behind the various design descisions taken in the prototype website for the Australian Red Cross Lifeblood. Correspondingly, the various iterations and changes of the website prototype will be documented; aided by the explanation of fundamental design descisions that had led the designers towards making those particular changes.

Reflection on Website Design Proposal

The intended website prototype will effectively use the final mockups (ideated within the website design proposal) as a reference for which it is created upon. Correspondingly, feedback gained previously from the website design proposal itself (through tutors and users) will be also applied as an iteration through the intended website prototype. However, it is notable that the process of feedback and improvement is an ongoing iterative process in which the designers intend to constantly collect and recieve user feedback in order to be analysed and applied to the final intended web prototype for the Australian Red Cross Lifeblood.

Landing Page

The landing page of the Australian Red Cross Lifeblood proposal website had been intended to give users an effective and clear first impression of what the proposal website had to offer to the user in whole. Hence, the designers intentions behind creating the landing page was to be as simple as possible through limiting the amount of elements of the page. Correspondingly, the colors and font across the landing page had been consistent as inspired by the web proposal documentation mood board.

Iterations Between the Mockups and the Final Prototype

The overall visual style and aesthetic of the landing page across both the mockup and final prototype had stayed consistent with very minimal change. The largest difference between the mockup and final prototype had included:

  • Condensation of pages: External pages such as the contact us page and the social media page had been removed and thus included within the landing page instead. This design descision had been justified to give the user the easiest possible usability within the website by keeping the amount of HTML pages to a minimum.
  • Consistent navigation bar: What had previously been a hamburger button in the mobile version of the ACRL website has now been removed. Instead, the navigation bar across both desktop and mobile are now the same as it is visually appealing and works extremely well on all devices.

    Iterations Between the Mockups and the Final Prototype

    The overall visual style and aesthetic of the blood details page in the prototype had stayed mainly similar, hence having very minimal change compared to the mockup. However, there were some noticable differences of which had included:

    • The removal of the progress bar: The progress bar had been mainly removed as it had served no real purpose for the final website prototype. According to user feedback, it was notable that the blood donation and eligibility process had been so short, that were was absolutely no need for a progress bar; particularly for such a short process.
    • Addition of more content: While the content of the mockup had been transferred to the website prototype, the designers felt that it was necessary to add more content such as statistics and vital blood donation details that would serve beneficial to the whole blood donation process. Correspondingly, designers felt that information such as "experiences of blood donators" and "how your donation works" were all content that users needed to know and acknowledge before embarking upon a blood donation process

    Eligibility Criteria Page

    The eligibility criteria page of the Australian Red Cross Lifeblood prototype website had been intended to inform the user of the physical and health requirements a user must meet in order to effectively donate blood. Hence, through the use of various design principles, the designers aim of the website was to make the eligibility information and criteria to be as easily comprehended as possible. Correspondingly, the designers wanted no error when users had to determine whether they were eligible or ineligible to donate blood. Hence these intentions had been achieved through using easy unordered lists of criteria that users can follow and apply themselves to. Correspondingly, a eligiblity form could be filled out for users who were unusre and confused about their eligiblity status or just wanted to confirm their eligibility status as well.

    Eligible CTA Page

    The eligible call-to-action page of the Australian Red Cross Lifeblood website prototype is the final step of the blood donation process for users who are deemed physically able and eligible to donate blood. Through this, the intent of the page was to allow users to enter their details for an ACRL representative to contact them and thus carry out the final physical step of the blood donation process to actually donate blood. Hence, this had been facillitated through a simple form where users can enter their details and submit.
